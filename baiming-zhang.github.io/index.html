Baiming Zhang （张柏铭）

I'm a junior undergraduate in Engineering Mechanics at Zhejiang University, affiliated with the Chu Kochen Honors College. My research interests include scientific machine learning, neural operators, computational fluid dynamics, and PDE-related learning frameworks. I am currently working under the guidance of Prof. Shiying Xiong.

My recent work spans neural PDE learning, scientific computing, and physically inspired machine learning methods, with applications to fluid systems, Schrödinger-type models, and reduced-order modeling.

Previously, I also worked on quantum and atomic physics related topics, including Rydberg atoms, Raman effects, EIT, and related theoretical modeling.

Research

My research focuses on machine learning methods for physical systems, especially data-driven approaches to partial differential equations, operator learning, and structure-aware modeling. I am particularly interested in how local representations, physical constraints, and efficient architectures can be combined to improve generalization, stability, and interpretability.

Lightweight Local Neural Operator Learning for PDEs
Independent first-author work, manuscript in preparation

Leading a research project on lightweight local-to-global neural operator learning. The work explores efficient local representations and reformulation strategies for improving the stability and interpretability of learning frameworks for partial differential equation related tasks.

Neural Learning for Incompressible Schrödinger Flow
Second author, manuscript in preparation

Participating in a project on neural network based learning of incompressible Schrödinger flow, with an emphasis on unified perspectives connecting fluid-like dynamics and quantum-inspired systems.

Reduced-Order Fluid Modeling with Energy Minimization and Implicit Neural Fields
Concept-stage first-author project

Developing ideas for a reduced-order fluid simulation framework that combines energy-based modeling and implicit neural representations. This direction is intended for future collaboration with the Dynamic Graphics Project (DGP), University of Toronto.
